## 2. Wiley AI disclosure 2026 — required language

**Source:** [Wiley AI Guidelines](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/publish/book/resources/ai-guidelines/) + [2025 release announcement](https://newsroom.wiley.com/press-releases/press-release-details/2025/Wiley-Releases-AI-Guidelines-for-Authors/default.aspx)

### What requires disclosure

- AI used to **develop substantive content** (arguments, analysis, sourcing, structure)
- AI used to generate text that survives in the final manuscript

### What does NOT require disclosure

- Grammar/spelling check
- General editing
- Translation between languages (per common interpretation)

### Where to put the disclosure

Three valid locations (in order of Wiley preference):
1. **Methods section** (if applicable) — strongest position
2. **Acknowledgements** — standard for theoretical papers like B1
3. **Preliminary statement** — at start of paper, before main text

### Recommended disclosure text for B1 (modeled on the BioSystems v0.4.13 disclosure that passed)

```
AI Use Declaration

During the preparation of this work, the author used AI-assisted tools
(Claude Opus, GPT, Gemini) to support manuscript organization,
clarity checks, and language editing of selected passages. After
using these tools, the author reviewed, edited, and verified the
content of the manuscript, including all argumentation, citations,
historical claims, and final wording. The author takes full
responsibility for the content of the article.
```

### Critical Wiley restriction (NOT in BioSystems version)

> "Authors must not use any AI Technology that restricts their, Wiley's, or any other party's use of the Material."

This means: **don't use AI tools whose ToS claim training rights on submitted content**. Claude/GPT/Gemini are fine on standard plans (no training rights claimed); ChatGPT free tier is more ambiguous. The disclosure should implicitly or explicitly confirm use of "tools that do not retain rights to the submitted content." Add as an extra sentence if you want to be airtight:

```
The AI tools used were configured to ensure that no provider gained
any rights over the author's underlying content, including any right
to train models on the manuscript.
```
