## The paper, made explicit

This document is **Version B1** of the essay, reframed for submission to *Systems Research and Behavioral Science* (Wiley). The title has been transformed into **"Relational Exit as a Structural Condition of Viability in Multi-Level Cooperative Systems"** — a "systemic mask" that enters through viability rather than through the political question.

The underlying argument is identical to v1.3, but the text undergoes several strategic transformations:

- The term "ontological antifascism" **disappears entirely** from the title and body
- Libertarian vocabulary (NAP, property, secession) is replaced by neutral equivalents: "protected space", "boundary integrity", "non-aggression" as *design principles*
- Crypto-native references (Moloch DAO, Steem → Hive) reduced to a sober mention in the discussion
- The framing shifts from "what makes totalitarianism possible?" to "what structural conditions must a cooperative system satisfy in order to remain viable?"

The principal contribution: the concept of **relational exit** — the exit morphism does not require spatial displacement but only cessation of the predatory relationship. It is a "change of relation, not a change of place".

## Gains relative to v1.3

### §5.1 Non-heteronomisation

Significantly more developed. The distinction between **admissible** constraint (which operates *through* the sub-system's organisation) and **non-admissible** constraint (which *replaces* that organisation) is a real conceptual contribution, only sketched in v1.3. It answers the objection "if non-heteronomisation forbids all constraint, it is anarchist by definition". No: it forbids the destruction of the capacity for self-production, not asymmetric interaction.

### §6.2 Transparency about limits

The acknowledgement that Proposition 2 "has been characterised as potentially close to a restatement of the definitions" is a rare gesture of honesty. The asymmetry between the three instantiations is explicitly recognised. An author who flags their own weaknesses at this level of detail inspires more trust than one who conceals them.

### Integration of Hirschman

Substantially improved. Hirschman's "radicalisation" is articulated in two movements: exit moves from a mechanism of protest to a structural condition of non-pathology, and its most accomplished form moves from flight to relational dissociation.

## Persistent or new fragilities

### The epistemic problem of the "mask"

The submitted paper says "this paper does not offer a complete political theory", whereas the research programme claims precisely to be founding a political theory. A reader who knows both versions might see this as a form of strategic under-declaration of the real ambition.

### The diagnostic → design gap

"Knowing that exit must be guaranteed does not tell us *how* to guarantee it in a specific institutional context." For SRBS, where the audience expects design implications, this gap will be felt more harshly than in a philosophy journal.

### Absence of Beer (VSM)

The absence of any confrontation with Beer's Viable System Model is a notable strategic oversight for SRBS. Beer diagnosed organisational viability; Keo diagnoses the viability of any multi-level cooperative architecture — the link is natural and reviewers will expect it.

## Scope and implications

### Academic strategy

The coexistence of three versions (A: militant foundation paper, B1: systemic mask, B2: philosophical mask) is a very conscious illustration of how a single thesis must be reframed to cross disciplinary boundaries. A remarkable object of sociology of knowledge.

### Contribution in systems science

Three contributions that the literature does not exactly provide: a categorical formalisation of the distributed/monopolistic predation distinction, the concept of relational exit as a displacement of Hirschman, and a unification of cancer/totalitarianism/ecological invasion under the same formal invariant. For an SRBS reader familiar with Beer's VSM, it is a natural complement — Keo's diagnostic tool is sharper (a single criterion: exit) but less operational.

### Paradoxical effect of the political reframing

On the one hand, it makes the argument more receivable by depoliticising the surface. On the other, it partially conceals the radical scope: if in situ exit is the condition of viability, then **any political structure claiming a territorial monopoly without an individual right of secession is structurally pathological** in the sense of ThP — which includes nearly all existing states. v1.3 assumed this implication. B1 masks it under "governance design principles" — strategically necessary but intellectually less courageous.

## Foreseeable objections from the SRBS referee

1. Is Proposition 2 really a theorem or a restatement?
2. Is the political instantiation sufficiently formalised to go beyond analogy?
3. Where is the constructive design — the paper diagnoses but does not build
4. Why no confrontation with Beer's Viable System Model?
